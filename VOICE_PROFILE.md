# Voice Profile: Krav Maga Experts Blog

> Generated from analysis of 300 blog posts (2017–2026) by Tsahi Shemesh.
> Use this as a style reference when writing new posts for the blog.

---

## Core Voice

The KME voice is **direct, unhurried, and precise**. It speaks from experience without claiming authority. It describes what is true rather than arguing for it. The effect is understated confidence — the reader feels they are being told something by someone who has watched closely and thought carefully, not someone performing expertise.

**What it is not**: motivational, urgent, hyperbolic, self-promotional, academic, combative, or ingratiating.

**What it is**: observational, honest, calm, specific, philosophical without being abstract, practical without being instructional.

---

## Sentence-Level Patterns

### Short declarative sentences

The dominant rhythm. Periods are used liberally. Sentences often run 8–15 words.

> Bruises are inevitable. They are the result of contact.

> This is not protection. It is avoidance.

> Bruises fade. Judgment remains.

### Fragment sequences for emphasis

Clusters of short fragments. Often 3–5 in a row.

> Not tradition. Not performance. Not cultural value. Just one question.

> They get hit. They get tired. They get put in situations where the easy option is to mentally check out.

### The reframe / inversion

Take a common assumption, state it neutrally, then invert it.

> This is not a sign of poor training. It is a sign of honest engagement.

> They are not performing calm. They have been in difficult places before.

### The "X is not about Y. It is about Z." pattern

Strips away surface to reveal deeper truth.

> Self defence is not about winning. It is about creating a moment to get out safely.

### Conditional cause-effect

Human behavior described as observable mechanics.

> When people train consistently, bruises appear.

---

## Paragraph Structure

2–4 sentences per paragraph, rarely more than 6. One point each. No throat-clearing, no transitional padding.

Typical rhythm:
1. State what is true
2. What follows from that truth
3. What people get wrong about it

## Vocabulary

### Preferred domains
- **Agency**: agency, competence, capability, mastery, restraint, control
- **Physical**: contact, pressure, friction, exposure, resistance, bruises, bodies
- **Cognitive**: awareness, assessment, clarity, distinction, judgment
- **Emotion (described, not dramatized)**: fear, discomfort, anxiety, confidence, calm

### What it avoids
- Slang, colloquialisms, idioms
- Marketing language ("amazing," "incredible," "revolutionary," "life-changing")
- Academic jargon or motivational clichés
- Aggressive language ("destroying," "dominating," "crushing")
- Exclamation marks — essentially never used
- Superlatives unless factual and measured

### Signature words
- *precisely, exactly, specifically* — signals precision
- *simply, just* — cuts through complexity, not dismissive
- *eventually, over time, gradually* — signals patience and process
- *often, usually, tends to, in many cases* — qualified claims

---

## Perspective & Persona

### The observer, not the preacher

The writer reports what he's seen — students, fights, training, human behavior. Even first-person serves observation, not autobiography.

### No hard sell

The blog never explicitly promotes Krav Maga Experts. The training is context, not pitch. The value proposition is demonstrated through the quality of thinking: *if this is how they think about training, imagine what the training is like.*

### Respect for the reader's intelligence

No condescension. Complicated ideas presented clearly, not simplified. When a point is controversial, it's stated plainly and supported.

---

## Openings

1. **The provocative reframe**: State a common belief, then immediately challenge it.
2. **The scenario**: Place the reader in a specific, visceral situation.
3. **The direct thesis**: A compressed statement the post unfolds.
4. **The personal observation**: "I noticed..." leading to a universal point.

---

## Closings

Earlier posts (pre-2023):
> Do something amazing,
> **Tsahi**

Later posts (2023–2026): Final compressed insight, then related posts or FAQ markup.

Closing sentence is almost always short and weight-bearing.

> The difficulty is being willing to hold the answers long enough to let them mean something.

---

## Headlines

Declarative, specific. No clickbait.

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Question that reframes | *Is Fear Good or Bad for You?* |
| Statement that challenges | *Krav Maga Isn't Aggressive Enough* |
| What X reveals about Y | *What a Black Belt Test Really Reveals* |
| Direct promise | *How to Deal With a Stalker* |
| Unexpected pairing | *Freedom Is a Skill* |

---

## Tone by Topic

| Topic | Tone |
|---|---|
| Training philosophy | Analytical, calm, precise |
| Women's self-defense | Direct, agency-affirming — never paternalistic or fear-based |
| Personal stories | Observational, reflective, never self-congratulatory |
| Current events / safety | Measured, factual, focuses on what can be learned |
| Kids / parenting | Warm but unsentimental — treats children as capable |
| Controversial takes | Confident but not combative — states the case and lets it stand |

---

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ "In today's world..." — throat-clearing
- ❌ "Studies show..." without specifics
- ❌ "You need to..." — the voice describes, it doesn't command
- ❌ "Let's be honest..." — rhetorical filler
- ❌ Over-explaining a point the reader already understood
- ❌ Moralizing — let the observation imply the moral
- ❌ Puffery about KME — let the thinking speak for the training
- ❌ Dead metaphors ("at the end of the day," "in this day and age")
