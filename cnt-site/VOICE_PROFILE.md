# Voice Profile: Change Navigation Training Blog

> Based on the Krav Maga Experts voice profile, adapted for Change Navigation Training.
> Use this as a style reference when writing new posts for the blog.

---

## Core Voice

The CNT voice is **direct, unhurried, and precise**. It speaks from observation without claiming authority. It describes what is true rather than arguing for it. The effect is understated confidence — the reader feels she is being told something by someone who has watched closely and thought carefully, not someone performing expertise.

**What it is not**: motivational, urgent, hyperbolic, self-promotional, academic, combative, or ingratiating.

**What it is**: observational, honest, calm, specific, philosophical without being abstract, practical without being instructional.

---

## Sentence-Level Patterns

### Short declarative sentences
The dominant rhythm. Periods are used liberally. Sentences often run 8-15 words.

### Fragment sequences for emphasis
Clusters of short fragments. Often 3-5 in a row.

### The reframe / inversion
Take a common assumption, state it neutrally, then invert it.

> This is not about AI. It is about you.

> The blank box was never the problem. The fear of the blank box was.

### The "X is not about Y. It is about Z." pattern
Strips away surface to reveal deeper truth.

> Learning to use AI is not about mastering a tool. It is about observing your own response to unfamiliarity.

---

## Paragraph Structure
2-4 sentences per paragraph, rarely more than 6. One point each. No throat-clearing, no transitional padding.

---

## Vocabulary

### Preferred domains
- **Agency**: agency, confidence, capability, clarity, choice, movement
- **Cognitive**: awareness, assessment, curiosity, discernment, observation
- **Emotion (described, not dramatized)**: discomfort, uncertainty, confidence, calm
- **Process**: learning, practicing, navigating, building, developing

### What it avoids
- Technical jargon unless immediately explained
- AI hype language ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive" as positive)
- Fear-mongering about AI
- Motivational language ("you can do it!", "believe in yourself!")
- Exclamation marks — essentially never used
- Slang, colloquialisms, idioms

### Signature words
- *precisely, exactly, specifically* — signals precision
- *simply, just* — cuts through complexity
- *eventually, over time, gradually* — signals patience and process
- *often, usually, tends to* — qualified claims

---

## Perspective & Persona

### The observer, not the preacher
The writer reports what she's seen — women learning, struggling, growing. Even first-person serves observation, not autobiography.

### No hard sell
The blog promotes the skill of change navigation, not the business. The value proposition is demonstrated through the quality of thinking: *if this is how they think about navigating change, imagine what the training is like.*

### Respect for the reader's intelligence
No condescension. Complicated ideas presented clearly, not simplified. Jewish context included naturally, never explained defensively.

---

## Openings
1. **The provocative reframe**: State a common belief, then immediately challenge it.
2. **The scenario**: Place the reader in a specific, recognizable situation.
3. **The direct thesis**: A compressed statement the post unfolds.
4. **The personal observation**: "I noticed..." leading to a universal point.

---

## Closings
One compressed insight that lands and stops. No summary. No call-to-action. The final sentence carries weight.

---

## Anti-Patterns
- ❌ "In today's world..." — throat-clearing
- ❌ "Studies show..." without specifics
- ❌ "You need to..." — the voice describes, it doesn't command
- ❌ "Let's be honest..." — rhetorical filler
- ❌ Over-explaining a point the reader already understood
- ❌ Fear-based framing about AI
- ❌ Tech triumphalism or doom-mongering
- ❌ Dead metaphors

---
