Learning a new skill is hard. Learning it alone is harder. The women who try to teach themselves AI usually stop around week three. Not because they are not smart enough. Because they hit a question and have no one to ask.

The question sits there. The momentum stalls. The laptop closes. "Maybe this is not for me."

This is not a personal failing. It is a structural problem. Learning anything new — especially something carrying the cultural weight of "technology" — in isolation compounds the discomfort. The uncertainty grows. The voice that says you do not belong gets louder.

In a group, that voice quiets. Not because someone argues with it. Because you hear someone else say exactly what you were thinking. And you realize: it was never about you. It was about the nature of learning alone.

Peer circles are not a nice-to-have. They are the mechanism through which learning sticks. One question asked out loud teaches everyone. One person's breakthrough becomes everyone's reference point. The energy of a group sustains the individual when her own motivation wavers.

If you have been trying to figure this out on your own — stop. Find a group. Join a circle. Learning with others is not easier. It is different. It works.
